We have two events, and we want to know the probability that either event occurs. $$ \begin{aligned} P(A\or B) &= P(A) + P(B) - P(A\and B) \\ &= P(A) + P(B) - P(A)\cdot P(B|A) \leftarrow \text{Rule of multiplication} \end{aligned} $$ More than 2 probabilities results in a multiplicative affect on the rule: $$ P(A\or B\or C) = P(A) + P(B) + P(C) - P(A\and B) - P(A\and C) - P(B\and C) + P(A\and B\and C) $$ ## Negation Rule Negating a probability results in the probability that that event does *not* occur. $$ P(A) = 1 - P(A') \\ P((A\or B)') = P(A'\and B') $$ ## Law of Total Probability Given $A_1, A_2, A_3, ...$ as mutually exclusive and mutually exhaustive events, and B as any event: $$ \begin{aligned} P(B) &= P(A_1\and B) + P(A_2\and B) + \ldots + P(A_k\and B) \\ &= P(A_1)P(B|A_1) + P(A_2)P(B|A_2) + \ldots + P(A_k)P(B|A_k) \end{aligned} $$ This is the Law of Total Probability, which allows us to change given probabilities from subsets into a probability applied to the encompasing set. Said otherwise, if we're given a probability in terms of a subset (eg $P(A|B)$) we can change that to a probability in terms of the entire set (eg $P(A\and B)$). For example, we can change $P(A|B)$ into $P(B|A)$. This is extremely important and very useful. $$ P(A_j|B) = \frac{P(A_j\and B)}{P(B)} = \frac{P(A_j)P(B|A_j)}{\sum^N_{k=0} P(A_k)P(B|A_k)} \\ \text{where, } N = \text{the number of A's} $$ # Sources Rule of Addition & Multiplcation: [Stattrek](https://stattrek.com/probability/probability-rules.aspx#:~:text=The%20probability%20that%20Events%20A%20or%20B%20occur%20is%20the,A%20and%20B%20are%20dependent) Everything Else: OSU STAT Department
